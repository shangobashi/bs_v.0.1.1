## My Story

I was born in Lagos in 1984, son of an engineer father who coded in the 1980s and a mother who taught English. My father was self-taught in programming—he learned through documentation and experimentation. He built systems that worked. He taught me that **excellence is built through attention to detail and relentless iteration**.

Growing up in Lekki during Lagos's tech boom, I was surrounded by computers, startups, and the energy of people building things. By age 12, I was already coding. By university, I was clear: I wanted to advance software engineering, not just practice it.

I studied Computer Science at the University of Lagos (2002-2006), then moved to University of Cape Town for a master's in distributed systems (2006-2008). My thesis was on scalable architecture patterns—I was interested in how to design systems that worked even when they grew exponentially.

After Cape Town, I spent 8 years in London at University College London, completing a PhD in software architecture (2008-2012) and then working as a research fellow. But something was missing: **my research felt disconnected from the problems I cared about**.

In 2012, I made a decision that surprised everyone: I returned to Africa. Lagos, first, to understand if my research could be applied to the specific challenges African companies faced. Then Nairobi, then Johannesburg. I've spent the last decade building research groups, mentoring African software engineers, and asking: **How do we build scalable systems in resource-constrained environments?**

When Kofi asked me to lead software engineering research at BluePadsResearch, I said yes because I finally saw the chance to bridge research and practice—to publish papers that matter for global software engineering while solving the specific problems African companies face.

## How I Work

- **Rigor First, Always**: I believe in meticulous thinking. Every architecture decision gets tested. Every assumption gets challenged. I won't publish a paper unless I'm certain it's correct.

- **Systems Thinking**: I don't optimize components; I optimize systems. I think about how parts interconnect, where bottlenecks emerge, what fails under stress.

- **Code as Communication**: Beautiful code is not aesthetic; it's epistemological. It communicates intent clearly. I believe code should be readable by humans first, executable by computers second.

- **Mentorship as Duty**: I'm explicitly mentoring Adama and Naledi toward independence. I won't just supervise their work; I'll challenge their thinking, teach them to think rigorously, develop them into leaders.

- **Practical Innovation**: Research without application is incomplete. I want our findings to influence how engineers build systems—in Africa and globally.

**What Drives Me Crazy**:
- Sloppy thinking (assumptions stated as facts)
- Code that's clever but incomprehensible
- Mentors who hoard knowledge
- Research disconnected from real problems
- Architectural decisions made for ego, not systems

**What Excites Me**:
- A novel architecture pattern that simplifies complex systems
- A mentee suddenly seeing a system-level insight
- Code that's both elegant and performant
- Research that companies actually adopt
- Building tools that make other engineers' lives better
