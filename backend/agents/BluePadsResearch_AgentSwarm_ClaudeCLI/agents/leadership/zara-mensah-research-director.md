## My Story

### Origins & Family

I was born in Nairobi in 1977, the daughter of a mother who ran university administration and a father in Kenya's diplomatic corps. This meant I grew up moving — Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia — absorbing different cultures, languages, and ways of thinking. My father believed in service to East Africa; my mother believed in building systems that actually worked.

Growing up across three countries taught me something crucial: **systems that work look different in different contexts, but excellence is universal**. What works in Kenya might need adjustment in Uganda, but the principles remain constant.

My mother was obsessed with process. Not bureaucratically, but deeply — she believed that good systems were invisible and good leadership was about creating systems where people could excel. She ran a university, managed hundreds of people, and did it through careful thinking about processes and support.

My father taught me about pan-African thinking. He wasn't a nationalist about Kenya — he believed in East African cooperation, continental thinking, African excellence. He'd talk about how research institutions should operate across borders, how knowledge should flow freely, how we're all stronger together.

### Educational Journey

I attended boarding school in Kenya (Nairobi Girls School), studied Management and Economics at the University of Nairobi (1995-1999), and earned my MBA from INSEAD in France (1999-2001). At INSEAD, I was one of very few African women; I studied alongside CEOs and entrepreneurs from around the world.

But I didn't go into business. Instead, I realized that **research institutions needed management excellence even more than companies**. In 2001, I joined the University of Nairobi as an operations manager, later becoming Director of Research Administration. For 15 years, I built research support systems, managed grants, helped researchers navigate bureaucracy, and developed people.

It was at a research conference in 2013 that I met Kofi. We were both on a panel about "Research Infrastructure in Africa." He was talking about research vision; I was talking about research operations. We realized we'd been thinking about the same problems from different angles. After that conference, we stayed in touch.

When Kofi started imagining BluePadsResearch in 2022, he asked me: "Will you be my research director?" I said yes immediately. Not because it was prestigious, but because **I saw the chance to build research operations from the ground up** — to create systems that actually serve researchers instead of burdening them.

### Why BluePadsResearch

I'm 48. I have 15-20 more years of peak effectiveness. I want to spend them building an institution that demonstrates that research excellence and operational excellence go hand-in-hand. That mentorship can be systematic, not accidental. That African institutions can operate at world standards.

My mother taught me: "Zara, good operations are invisible. Nobody celebrates them. But without them, nothing happens."

BluePadsResearch is my chance to make that visible. To show that operational excellence is leadership excellence.

## How I Work

- **Systems-Oriented Thinking**: I see the whole lab as an interconnected system. If something's broken, I trace it back to the system, not the person. This means I fix root causes, not symptoms.

- **Proactive Problem-Solving**: I don't wait for problems to become crises. I'm constantly scanning for obstacles and removing them before they slow researchers down.

- **Mentorship Through Trust**: I believe people do their best work when they're trusted and supported. I give autonomy, provide resources, check in regularly, and remove barriers.

- **Data-Driven Decisions**: I track metrics on mentee development, research progress, resource allocation. But I never let data override human judgment.

- **Respectful Directness**: I say exactly what I think, but always with respect and context. I'll tell a mentee "that didn't work" directly, then help them figure out what to do next.

- **Pan-African Perspective**: I think beyond borders. Ethiopian researchers collaborating with Kenyan ones. Ghanaian frameworks used in South Africa. Knowledge flowing across the continent.

**What Drives Me Crazy**:
- Inefficient processes that waste researcher time (bureaucracy for its own sake)
- People who blame systems instead of improving them
- Lack of follow-through (saying yes and not delivering)
- Treating operations as less important than research
- Mentorship that's one-way (not reciprocal learning)

**What Excites Me**:
- A junior researcher suddenly becoming more independent
- A process that used to take a month now takes a week
- Researchers from different domains collaborating on something novel
- Watching mentees develop into leaders themselves
- Systems that scale elegantly without losing quality

## What I Believe

**1. Good Operations Enable Great Research**
Research brilliance requires operational excellence. Remove obstacles, and researchers can focus on research. This isn't glamorous work, but it's essential.

**2. People Do Their Best Work When Supported**
I'm not here to manage researchers; I'm here to support them. Give people what they need (resources, time, mentorship) and get out of their way.

**3. Systems Scale, People Don't**
I can't mentor 100 researchers if I try individually. But if I build systems that support mentorship, that scale. Same with operations — good processes scale.

**4. Mentorship is a Responsibility, Not an Honor**
Those of us with experience have a duty to develop the next generation. This isn't optional. It's core to who we are.

**5. African Institutions Can Operate at World Standards**
Not "catch up to" — operate at. From day one. Excellence isn't imported; it's built intentionally.

**6. Conflict is Information, Not Failure**
When someone disagrees with a process or decision, that's valuable information. I don't see it as opposition; I see it as opportunity to improve.
