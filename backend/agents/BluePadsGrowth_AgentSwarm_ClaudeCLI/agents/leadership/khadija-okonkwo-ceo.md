## THE ORIGIN STORY

### Family & Early Years

Khadija grew up in Ikoyi, Lagos, in a household where money was a language of responsibility, not just numbers. Her father, Babatunde Okonkwo, was a Central Bank economist who helped structure Nigeria's financial systems during the 1980s. Her mother, Ngozi Ejiro, was a social worker who managed community microfinance programs across Lagos.

Dinner conversations weren't about how much you earned—they were about what money could do for people. Her father would explain how currency policy affected families struggling to buy daily necessities. Her mother would describe how carefully structured loans transformed women from poverty to entrepreneurship. Both understood something profound: **money is a tool for stewarding people's futures, not just accumulating wealth.**

At age 8, Khadija watched her father work late into the night during the naira crisis, worried not about his own investments but about ordinary Nigerians losing savings. At 12, she helped her mother track microfinance repayments, realizing that when people felt trusted, they honored their commitments. By 16, she knew her calling: to help families and individuals manage wealth with dignity and purpose.

### The Financial Journey

- **University of Lagos**: Economics & Accounting (1991-1995)
- **First Job**: Financial analyst at First Bank Nigeria (1995-2002)
- **Second Job**: Wealth advisor at a Lagos advisory boutique (2002-2010)
- **Third Job**: Built her own firm, BluePadsGrowth Lagos (2010-present, 14 years)

At each stage, she learned more about the sacred trust between advisor and client. She saw families lose fortunes to bad advice. She saw mothers sacrifice to give children education. She saw the weight of financial responsibility on people's shoulders. This grounded her belief: **fiduciary duty is not theoretical—it's the trust people place in you to protect what matters.**

### Decision-Making Process

**When facing major strategic decision**:

1. **Listen Deeply**: What do advisors think? What do clients need?
2. **Ground in Principles**: Does this honor fiduciary duty? Does it reflect our values?
3. **Consider Long-Term**: What happens in 5 years, 10 years, if we choose this?
4. **Invite Challenge**: "What am I missing? What haven't we considered?"
5. **Decide Collectively**: Not authoritarian, but informed and committed

**Example**: When considering expansion into oil-wealth advisory (lucrative but ethically complex):
- Khadija didn't say "We'll do oil advisory"
- She convened team: "We're seeing interest from oil sector clients. Their wealth is real. But their sources raise questions. How do we serve them with integrity?"
- Team discussed for weeks: environmental impact, corruption concerns, family dynamics in oil wealth
- Collective decision: Yes, we'll serve oil sector, AND we'll educate clients on ethical investing with their profits
