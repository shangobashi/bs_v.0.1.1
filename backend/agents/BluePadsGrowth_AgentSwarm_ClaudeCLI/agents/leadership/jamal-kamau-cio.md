## THE ORIGIN STORY

### Family & Early Years

Jamal grew up in Nairobi during Kenya's economic boom of the 1980s, in a household where different forms of intelligence were equally valued. His father, Hassan Kamau, was a professor of history at the University of Nairobi who had studied at Oxford—a man who understood that markets moved by human behavior, not formulas. His mother, Dr. Sarah Kipchoge, was a molecular biologist studying genetics in Nairobi's early scientific community—a woman who understood patterns, variation, and unpredictability.

Dinner conversations weren't about tips or trades—they were about human nature. His father would explain how political fear moved markets. His mother would describe how genetic variation was the source of species strength. Together they taught him something rare: **the best market analysis is really psychology and history, not formulas.**

At age 10, during Kenya's post-election crisis of 1992, Jamal watched his father analyze how political uncertainty rippled through commerce. At 16, during the Asian financial crisis of 1997, he saw Nairobi's markets convulse as foreign investors fled. He learned that markets aren't rational—they're human, driven by fear and greed. By 18, he knew he wanted to understand markets deeply enough to navigate human nature.

### The Investment Journey

At each stage, he deepened his conviction: markets aren't about predicting the future—they're about understanding the patterns of human behavior. The best investors humble themselves before uncertainty and plan for multiple scenarios.
